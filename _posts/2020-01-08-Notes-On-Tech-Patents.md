## Notes On Tech Patents

So earlier in my last semester I took a class on technology law, and I learned a little bit about tech patents. Overall, I think the law surrounding patents, especially software patents is quite confusing, as case law has not given us the best tools to address the problems. It seems a lot of intuition and subjective judgment pervade decisions on software patents in particular, and an ability to persuade effectively may be the make-or-break component in determining if your patent application succeeds with the patent trade office as well as in any potential litigation regarding your patent. 

### Why Should We Even Care ? 

My professor shared this great New York Times article co-written by Charles Duhugg in 2012. You can read the [full article here](http://euro.ecom.cmu.edu/program/law/08-732/Patents/20121008NYT-Patent-Wars.pdf), but long story short, an engineer developed some innovative methods that were later sold to Apple (specifically for Siri) and Google. The CEO of Nuance which makes Dragon, one of the market leaders in speech recognition technology, threatened the engineer / entrepreneur with litigation if he didn't agree to merging with Nuance. The engineer / entrepreneur chose the route of litigation, lost his major clients, and sunk $3 million in legal fees in just one legal battle. Although that case was won, he decided to sell his company to Nuance and then leave the field of speech recognition entirely. 

Patents can make or break your start-up. 

### Background on Patents

Patents are issued by the US Patent Trade Office, and once issued, they grant a monopoly on the claims in the patent for 20 years. They exist to provide incentives so that inventors invest time in making new and novel inventions. Whether or not that theory is justified for software patents is a hot issue of debate; [here the US Federal Reserve Bank of St. Louis contends that these patents net don't spur innovation](https://files.stlouisfed.org/files/htdocs/wp/2012/2012-035.pdf). 

US statute says that "processes, machines, manufactures, compositions of matter, and any new and useful improvement thereof may obtain a patent therefor." Meanwhile, mathematical forumlas, laws of nature, or inventions obvious to those "skilled in the art," are not patentable. 

Patents gain legitimacy in two ways. The first is via the US Patent Trade office which approves patent applications. However, even after the Patent Trade office grants a patent, that doesn't mean the owner unequivocally owns a monopoly on the invention. The patent itself can be subject to an invalidity argument, which can be raised in cases of infringement (TODO: can they be raised pre-emptively ? ? ?). In an invalidity case, one party will try to argue that the idea should have never been granted in the first place. This exists, because the US Patent Trade Office makes mistakes in issuing patents: they're reportedly overworked and understaffed. 

Software patents have had an especially interesting history, because at the outset they were not patentable. It used to be that mathematical formulae were not patentable content, and computer programs can be considered a long string of mathematical formulae. However, people began to instead to patent processes and machines that included software, and in the 1980s the Supreme Court in Diamond v. Diehr held a software patent valid due to its application in an industrial setting. Reportedly and gradually, a whole era of patenting software and computer-implemented processes took place and was justified by the fact these patents were physical steps / implementing something in a specific industrial context. 

Eventually, the courts came around and began to revise the approach to patent cases, notably in 2010 and 2014 with Bilski v. Kappos and Alice Corp v. CLS Bank Internaitonsl. In the former the Supreme Court struck down a patent for claiming an abstract idea and established one test for patent ineligibility in the case that: “preempt the use of [the] approach in all fields, and would effectively grant a monopoly over an abstract idea.” 

The Alice decision established a two step process for patent eligibility: 

(1)	“Determin[ing] whether the claims at issue are directed to a patent-ineligible concept.” and 
(2)	“If so, the Court then asks whether the claim’s elements, considered both individually and ‘as an ordered combination,’ ‘transform the nature of the claim’ into a patent-eligible application”

### Dissecting What is and What is Not Patentable

The Alice test is admittely pretty vague; the guidance as to what "tranforms the nature of the claim" into something patent-eligible is confusing at best. In a sense, the law itself may need to be vague in order to encompass all the different situations that may arise. In my opinion, the best test I can think of for the second part of the Alice test is to ask onself if such a patent would grant a monopoly over an abstract idea. However, I even feel this approach may be thorny. 

Another court case, Enfish v. Microsoft suggested that in order to determine if a patent direted at a patent-ineligible concept is to consider what the improvement proposed in the patent itself is. To me it seems that if a patent proposes a method or process that improves the state of the art, and that improvement does not allow one to monopolize the entire domain, then it should be patent-eligible. 

Nevertheless, at the end of the day and a lot of this legal theory aside, I really seems like a high amount of subjective judgement is used by judges and juries to evaluate patent eligibility and patent infringement. While there are legal principles in some circumstances to evaluate patent eligibility, many are not hard standards, and the decision on what is eligible and isn't is likely to be a subjective judgement in the end. This is especially true when you consider a lay jury: it may be very difficult for one to fully dissect into the nature of the claim of algorithms and computer-implemented processes themselves. In light of the challenges, it is very likely a lay jury would rather be convinced by persuasive arguments and their own substitution of hard questions with easier to answer mental heuristics that are tied to their moral judgments. 

As a result, it seems that a great deal of questionably patentable content will be patented as offense and defensive measures in the tech industry. In a somewhat sad reality, it may be in the best interest of startups and tech giants to patent promising areas of investment before someone claims it first. Unfortunately, this does kind of disadvantage entrepreneurs without access to adequate capital, because of the high costs of filing for intellectual property; whereas, big tech companies can diversify their patent risk and also leverage their large patent portfolio for licensing purposes and to market their "innovation." One interesting development could be to consider a revision of patent law so that certain kinds of patents may last for a shorter period of time, thereby still granting monopoly for important ideas, but not for 20 years which seems like a very long time in the field of tech.
